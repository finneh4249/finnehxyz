I want to move to Tokyo. There. Said it.

That probably sounds impulsive if you don't know me. If you do, it's probably the least surprising thing you've ever read.

Let me explain.

## Fourteen Years of Obsessive Transit Analysis

I don't know exactly when it started. Somewhere around 2012, I became genuinely obsessed with public transport networks.

Not in a "this is a nice way to get around" way. In a "I've read every Wikipedia article about the Tokyo Metro, I know the exact frequency of the Chuo Line during peak hours, and I can tell you why Melbourne's tram network is both the world's largest and deeply underutilised" way.

That obsession became analysis. I started mapping transit gaps, reading council advocacy documents, understanding mode share statistics, comparing infrastructure investment models across cities. I [wrote a 4,000-word breakdown of why Melbourne's West Gate Tunnel was a bad deal](/blog/westgate-discovery-day) while most people were just enjoying the Discovery Day event.

It's not a hobby. It's how I think.

And the city I keep coming back to, again and again, across fourteen years of reading and researching and analysing, is Tokyo.

## Why Tokyo Specifically

Tokyo's transit system is genuinely the best in the world. Not by one metric, not in one way — by basically every measure that matters.

The Tokyo Metro alone carries **8.7 million passengers per day**. Add in the Toei Subway, the JR lines, the private railways, the monorails, the shinkansen connections... you're looking at a network so interconnected and so precisely timed that train delays are measured in seconds, not minutes.

JR East publishes an annual punctuality report. In 2023, average delays on their urban network were **0.9 minutes**.

Melbourne's train network targets "on time" as anything within 5.5 minutes of schedule.

That gap is not a coincidence. It's not culture. It's a policy and investment outcome. And it's the kind of outcome that requires the kind of coordination, data analysis, and system thinking that I've spent fourteen years learning about from the outside.

I want to be on the inside.

### The Data Infrastructure Is Genuinely World-Class

Tokyo is one of the most data-rich transit environments anywhere. The Tokyo Metropolitan Government publishes open transit data. The Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism releases detailed modal split statistics. Smart card data from Suica and PASMO generates one of the richest origin-destination matrices of any transit network in existence.

I've been analysing Melbourne transit data as an outsider, making do with what's publicly available, writing advocacy pieces, mapping gaps. That's genuinely valuable work. But it's work constrained by what I can access from the outside.

In Tokyo, the data infrastructure exists to do that analysis properly. The organisational infrastructure to act on it exists too — in ways it genuinely doesn't in Melbourne.

I want to be where the problem is actually solvable.

## The Language I Started in Year 7

I started studying Japanese in 2012. Year 7. I was twelve.

I can't give you a single clean reason why. Japan had always felt magnetised to me — the transit, the cities, the food, the game design, the vending machines that somehow always have exactly what you need. Japanese was just the obvious next step when I had the chance to take it as a school subject.

I stuck with it through high school. I let it drift after that. I picked it back up a few years ago.

Right now I'm sitting somewhere around **N5** — the most basic level of the JLPT. I can read hiragana and katakana fluently, handle basic kanji, hold a very elementary conversation. I can order food. I can navigate transit apps. I can understand the announcement when the Yamanote Line doors are closing.

What I can't do is move to Japan without putting serious, sustained work into the language. And I know that.

But here's what N5 actually means in practice: **I understand enough to know exactly how much I don't know, and I know exactly what I need to learn.**

I'm not treating Japanese as a hobby. I'm treating it as a professional requirement.

N5 to N4 is the jump I'm working on now. N3 is where functional business communication starts. I have a realistic picture of the timeline, and I'm not pretending otherwise.
